<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader type="REQUIREMENTS ENGINEERING" title="Defining the fitness ecosystem." />
  
  <WorkProse>
    In my Software Engineering studies at McMaster, I’ve learned that the most difficult part of building a system isn’t always the implementation—it’s the definition. ACME Run was a project focused entirely on the "Why" and the "How" before the "What."

    The goal was to architect a gamified fitness platform that combined curated running trails with real-time heart rate monitoring. However, instead of jumping straight into code, we focused on the rigorous discipline of Requirements Engineering: identifying unmet needs in the North American fitness market and translating them into a hardened technical blueprint.

  </WorkProse>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader type="DOMAIN MODELING" title="Dissecting the environment." />

  <WorkSplitContent subtitle="Understanding invariants and constraints">
    To build a reliable fitness solution, we first had to define the world in which it lives. This meant identifying the specific components, constraints, and assumptions of the fitness domain.

    We spent a significant portion of the project dissecting user behavior and environmental invariants. By formalizing these relationships in a Domain Model, we ensured that the system architecture would be rooted in reality—accounting for everything from hardware heart-rate variability to the spatial constraints of local running trails.

  </WorkSplitContent>

<WorkFeatureList>
  <WorkFeature title="Component Architecture">
    Defined the modular breakdown of the ACME Run system, ensuring that trial curation, user
    tracking, and reward logic were strictly decoupled for future scalability.
  </WorkFeature>
  <WorkFeature title="Activity Logic">
    Engineered formal usage scenarios for Activity Runs, mapping out the exact sequence of data flow
    between heart rate sensors, GPS coordinates, and the gamification engine.
  </WorkFeature>
  <WorkFeature title="API Interfacing">

<WorkSection>
  <WorkRetrospective title="Architecture Over Code">
    ACME Run was a foundational learning experience that fundamentally changed my approach to development. It taught me that a well-documented requirement is just as valuable as a well-written function.

    By spending the time to deeply understand user needs and system invariants, I realized that many "coding problems" are actually just "requirement gaps" that could have been solved with a better blueprint. This academic grounding has made me a much more intentional developer, pushing me to think through the architecture of a system long before I open my IDE.

  </WorkRetrospective>
