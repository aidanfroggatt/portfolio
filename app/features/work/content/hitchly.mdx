<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="The Motivation" type="Context" />
  <WorkSplitContent subtitle="The Spark">
    Our journey began with a simple observation: university life is expensive. We found many students spending over $250 a month on parking and fuel alone.

    Beyond the cost, we wanted to address the environmental impact—single-occupancy vehicles heading to campus account for roughly 36% of our school's GHG emissions. Hitchly was our attempt to build a community around shared, sustainable travel.

  </WorkSplitContent>
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="The Commuter's Dilemma" type="Context" />
  <WorkEvolution
    contextHeading="The Trust Gap"
    context={
      <>
        Existing options felt fragmented. Public transit was unreliable for those outside main
        corridors, and traditional ridesharing was too expensive for a student budget. While people
        wanted to carpool, the "trust gap" on anonymous boards was too wide to bridge.
      </>
    }
    evolutionHeading="A Trusted Circle"
    evolution={
      <>
        We realized trust was our most important "feature." By restricting the platform to verified
        school emails and integrating professional identity checks, we created a closed-loop
        ecosystem where every rider and driver is a peer.
      </>
    }
  />
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="The Strategy" type="Product" />
  <WorkSubSection title="Our Foundational Pillars">
    <WorkFeatureList>
      <WorkFeature title="Verified Community">
        A strict school-domain requirement ensures every user is an accountable member of the
        community—whether a student, researcher, or professor.
      </WorkFeature>
      <WorkFeature title="Safety First">
        We integrated secure identity verification for drivers. It was a rigorous technical step,
        but essential for maintaining peace of mind in every car.
      </WorkFeature>
      <WorkFeature title="Fair Cost-Sharing">
        Our system calculates fair splits based on actual fuel and maintenance costs, moving away
        from profit-driven models to keep commuting affordable.
      </WorkFeature>
    </WorkFeatureList>
  </WorkSubSection>
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="Designing for Growth" type="Engineering" />
  <WorkProse>
    Knowing this system would need to scale, we adopted a modular architecture. By breaking 
    the application into independent "services"—like separate modules for matching, 
    payments, and profiles—we could work in parallel without bottlenecks. This 
    "separation of concerns" meant we could refine our matching logic without 
    risking the integrity of our security and identity systems.
  </WorkProse>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="The Heart of the App: Matching" type="Logic" />
  <WorkSplitContent subtitle="Alignment over Proximity">
    Effective matching is about more than proximity; it's about schedule alignment. A 
    rider living two minutes away is a poor match if their class starts an hour later.

    We developed a weighted algorithm that prioritizes timing and location, while
    also considering social preferences to ensure the morning commute is as
    pleasant as it is efficient.

  </WorkSplitContent>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="Learning Through Testing" type="Validation" />
  <WorkProse>
    Handling personal data required rigorous testing. Running hundreds of test cases taught us the
    importance of reliability over speed. We prioritized high code coverage to ensure the system
    gracefully handled edge cases—like blurry verification documents or mid-booking session
    timeouts.
  </WorkProse>
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader title="Looking Ahead" type="Future" />
  <WorkProse>
    While built for our campus, the architecture is designed to be "plug-and-play" for other
    universities. Future iterations include real-time GPS updates and smarter coordination tools to
    help students manage the inevitable last-minute schedule changes of university life.
  </WorkProse>
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkRetrospective title="Modularity for Security">
    The biggest challenge wasn't just the code, but the responsibility of data privacy. By moving
    our matching logic into an isolated, dedicated module, we learned how to balance product
    flexibility with system security. It allowed us to fine-tune the user experience without ever
    touching the sensitive security layers—a design principle I'll carry into every future project.
  </WorkRetrospective>
</WorkSection>
