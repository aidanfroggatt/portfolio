<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader type="ALGORITHMIC ART" title="The logic of organic terrain." />
  
  <WorkProse>
    In my software engineering courses at McMaster, one of the most interesting challenges was bridging the gap between theoretical data structures and visual output. I wanted to explore how procedural generation could create organic, believable landscapes using nothing but math and code.

    The Island Generator project was my attempt to move away from simple square grids. Real nature isn't made of pixels; it’s irregular and jagged. To replicate that, I had to dive into computational geometry and figure out how to manage a mesh that could represent the complex interconnectedness of terrain.

  </WorkProse>
</WorkSection>

<WorkSection>
  <WorkHeader type="ALGORITHMS" title="Pathfinding across polygons." />

  <WorkSplitContent subtitle="Navigating the abstract">
    Once the terrain was generated, the next challenge was traversal. Because the mesh wasn't a standard grid, traditional coordinate-based pathfinding wouldn't work. I had to implement graph-traversal algorithms that understood the adjacency list of the mesh.

    This allowed the system to calculate logical paths—such as the most efficient route for a capital city's supply line—while taking the generated elevation and terrain type into account. It was a practical lesson in how graph theory underpins modern navigation systems.

  </WorkSplitContent>

<WorkInsight title="Lessons in Visualization">
  The Island Generator wasn't just about making pretty maps; it was about learning how to debug
  invisible systems. When working with complex mesh neighbors, errors are rarely obvious in the
  code—they only become apparent when a path breaks or a polygon overlaps incorrectly. Building
  custom debug visualizers to see the underlying "skeleton" of the mesh taught me the importance of
  internal tooling. It showed me that as software grows in complexity, the ability to see what the
  data is doing is just as important as the logic itself. It was a humble reminder that even the
  most complex organic results are often built on a foundation of very strict, simple rules.
</WorkInsight>
